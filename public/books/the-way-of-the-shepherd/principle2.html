The Way of the Shepherd Principle 2: Know the Condition of Your Flock

Published on: October 25, 2023 by Henson M. Sagorsor

Leadership isn’t about barking orders from the sidelines. It’s about stepping into the trenches and truly understanding the people you lead. Did you know that 85% of employees feel more motivated when their leaders show genuine interest in their well-being? That’s not just a statistic—it’s a wake-up call.

In The Way of the Shepherd, Principle 2 reminds us: “Know the condition of your flock.” This isn’t about surface-level check-ins or generic team meetings. It’s about diving deep, asking the right questions, and listening—not just to what’s said, but to what’s left unsaid. When you know your team’s strengths, struggles, and aspirations, you don’t just manage them; you inspire them to thrive.

Here’s the kicker: knowing your flock isn’t a one-time task. It’s an ongoing commitment. It’s about creating a culture where trust is the foundation and communication flows both ways. Imagine the impact of a leader who not only knows their team’s goals but also their fears and frustrations. That’s where real transformation happens.

In this blog, we’ll break down actionable strategies to master this principle. From conducting meaningful one-on-ones to fostering psychological safety, you’ll walk away with tools to elevate your leadership game. Because when you know the condition of your flock, you don’t just lead—you shepherd.

Why Knowing Your Flock Matters

Great leaders don’t just manage tasks—they lead people. And to lead effectively, you need to understand what drives your team. Research shows that teams with leaders who actively engage with their members experience 21% higher productivity and 41% lower turnover rates. These numbers aren’t just impressive; they’re a testament to the power of connection.

When you take the time to know your flock, you’re not just building a team—you’re building trust. Trust is the cornerstone of any successful team, and it starts with understanding. Whether it’s knowing who thrives under pressure or who needs a little extra encouragement, these insights allow you to tailor your leadership approach for maximum impact.

How to Apply Principle 2 in Your Leadership

Applying The Way of the Shepherd Principle 2 requires intentionality and consistency. Here are actionable steps to help you get started:

1. Conduct Regular One-on-Ones

One-on-one meetings are a powerful tool for understanding your team members on a deeper level. Schedule these meetings regularly—not just when problems arise. Use this time to ask open-ended questions like:

  • What’s been your biggest win this week?
  • What challenges are you currently facing?
  • How can I better support you in your role?

These conversations build trust and give you valuable insights into your team’s condition.

2. Foster Psychological Safety

Psychological safety is the belief that one can speak up without fear of punishment or humiliation. Create an environment where your team feels safe to share their thoughts, ideas, and concerns. Encourage open dialogue and actively listen without judgment. When your team knows they can be honest, you’ll gain a clearer picture of their condition.

3. Observe and Engage

Leadership isn’t just about what’s said—it’s about what’s observed. Pay attention to non-verbal cues like body language, tone of voice, and energy levels. Are team members enthusiastic or disengaged? Do they collaborate well or seem isolated? Use these observations to guide your interactions and provide targeted support.

4. Tailor Your Leadership Approach

No two team members are the same. Some thrive on autonomy, while others need more guidance. Use what you learn about your flock to adapt your leadership style. For example:

  • Provide clear direction for those who need structure.
  • Offer creative freedom for those who excel with independence.
  • Recognize and celebrate individual achievements to boost morale.

By tailoring your approach, you’ll empower each team member to perform at their best.
