Seek First to Understand, Then to Be Understood

Published on: February 6, 2025 by Henson M. Sagorsor

Why Listening First is a Game Changer

Imagine sitting in a meeting where everyone is eager to speak, but no one is actually listening. Frustrating, right? Studies show that miscommunication costs businesses an average of $62.4 million per year due to errors, inefficiencies, and lost productivity. In the IT industry alone, misunderstandings in project requirements contribute to 70% of software failures.

In his book, The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People, Stephen Covey introduces a powerful mindset shift: "Seek First to Understand, Then to Be Understood." This habit is not just about communication—it’s about building trust, improving collaboration, and making better decisions in both professional and personal life.

Too often, we listen with the intent to reply, not to understand. We interrupt, assume, and mentally prepare our response before the speaker even finishes. But what if we truly listened first? What if we made people feel heard before offering our perspective? This is the foundation of effective communication, leadership, and problem-solving.

Whether you’re a team leader, IT professional, educator, or entrepreneur, mastering Habit 5 can help you resolve conflicts, enhance decision-making, and build deeper connections. The key? Empathic listening—the ability to fully understand others before expecting them to understand you.

The Power of Empathic Listening

Most people listen with the intent to reply, not to understand. We filter conversations through our own experiences, assuming we already know what the other person is saying. Stephen Covey calls this autobiographical listening—where we project our own thoughts, emotions, and perspectives instead of focusing on the speaker’s true message.

But what happens when we truly listen? We go beyond words. We grasp emotions, perspectives, and hidden concerns. This approach leads to stronger relationships, fewer misunderstandings, and better decision-making.

Studies show that leaders who actively listen improve workplace productivity by 40% and that companies with strong communication practices are 4.5 times more likely to retain top talent. Whether in IT, business, or daily life, mastering empathic listening can be the difference between success and failure.

Why Does This Matter in IT and Business?

  • IT Project Management: A developer misunderstands client needs and builds the wrong feature. Empathic listening would have prevented the miscommunication.
  • Leadership: A manager listens to an employee’s frustration without interrupting, helping to create an action plan that increases job satisfaction and performance.
  • Customer Support: A support agent who listens deeply instead of rushing to a script-based response resolves a user’s issue faster and improves client retention.

Empathic listening isn’t just a soft skill—it’s a critical success factor. Teams that prioritize active listening make fewer mistakes, collaborate better, and build trust with clients, employees, and stakeholders. It’s a game-changer in any industry.

How to Practice Empathic Listening

Empathic listening is more than just hearing words—it’s about understanding the speaker’s emotions, intent, and perspective. Stephen Covey emphasizes that true effective communication starts with listening first, ensuring that the real message is understood before responding. Here’s how to apply it in professional, personal, and relationship settings.

Step 1: Give Your Full Attention

In today’s fast-paced world, distractions are everywhere. Effective communication requires undivided attention. If you’re in a conversation, avoid checking emails, multitasking, or pre-planning your response before the speaker finishes.

Professional Example: During a project meeting, if you're answering emails while a colleague explains a critical issue, you might miss key details, leading to costly mistakes.

Relationship Example: If your partner is expressing frustration about their day and you’re scrolling through your phone, they may feel unheard, even if you respond with, “I’m listening.”

Actionable Tip: When someone speaks, put aside distractions, make eye contact, and show engagement with small verbal affirmations like “I see” or “Go on.”

Step 2: Listen for Meaning, Not Just Words

Many people listen to respond instead of listening to understand. But true active listening means paying attention to tone, emotions, and underlying concerns.

Professional Example: A frustrated client says, “This software update is confusing.” Instead of assuming they need training, consider that they might be struggling with poor UX design, unclear documentation, or an unstable interface

Relationship Example: A friend says, “You never have time for me.” Instead of responding defensively, recognize that they may be expressing a need for more quality time together rather than blaming you outright.

Actionable Tip: Before reacting, ask yourself: “What is this person really trying to express?” Then, acknowledge it before offering a solution.

Step 3: Reflect Back What You Heard

Summarizing what you heard helps confirm understanding and prevents miscommunication. This is especially useful in software development, IT support, and personal relationships

Professional Example: A manager says, “We need to improve our system's response time.” Instead of assuming they mean a server upgrade, you could respond: “So, you’re concerned about latency, and we need to explore caching, database optimization, or better request handling?”

Relationship Example: A partner says, “I don’t feel like we’re as close as we used to be.” Instead of dismissing it, you could say: “So, you’re feeling like we don’t spend as much meaningful time together, and you’d like us to reconnect?”

Actionable Tip: After someone speaks, summarize what you understood: “Just to clarify, are you saying…?” This ensures alignment and builds trust.

Step 4: Ask Clarifying Questions

Instead of assuming you understand everything, ask open-ended questions to dig deeper. This reduces misunderstandings and leads to better solutions.

Professional Example: "Did you test the API?" (Too vague, invites a yes/no response) "What specific challenges did you face when testing the API?" (Encourages detailed responses)

Relationship Example: Instead of saying, “Are you mad at me?” which may get a defensive response, ask, “What’s on your mind?” or “Can you tell me what’s bothering you?” This opens the conversation instead of shutting it down.

Actionable Tip: Use "What," "How," or "Why" to encourage meaningful dialogue and deeper insights.

Step 5: Validate the Speaker’s Perspective

People want to feel heard. Even if you disagree, acknowledging their viewpoint fosters better discussions.

Professional Example: A junior developer expresses frustration over a project deadline. Instead of dismissing their concerns, say: “I see that this deadline feels overwhelming. Let’s discuss how we can break the tasks into smaller parts.”

Relationship Example: A family member says, “You don’t support me.” Instead of reacting with, “That’s not true,” respond with: “I hear that you’re feeling unsupported. Can you share what I can do to help?”

Actionable Tip: Before offering your opinion, restate their concern and acknowledge their perspective.

Applying Habit 5 in the IT Era

In today’s fast-paced technology landscape, professionals often rush to provide solutions before fully understanding the problem. But in IT, cybersecurity, and software development, this habit can lead to costly mistakes. The best IT leaders, developers, and engineers know that seeking first to understand is the foundation of success.

Think about user experience (UX) design. If developers don’t fully grasp what users need, the result is an interface that frustrates rather than helps. The same applies to IT support: Without active listening, technicians might offer generic fixes instead of addressing the actual issue.

Real-World IT Examples

Here’s how empathic listening makes a difference in tech and business:

  • Cybersecurity: A security breach often begins with miscommunication. Security analysts who listen to all stakeholders reduce risks by addressing core vulnerabilities instead of just patching symptoms.
  • Software Development: Agile teams thrive when developers actively listen to client needs. Misinterpreting requirements can lead to wasted resources and unnecessary revisions.
  • IT Support: A customer reports that "the system isn’t working." Instead of assuming, an experienced IT professional asks clarifying questions to understand whether it’s a hardware issue, software glitch, or user error.

Habit 5 isn’t just about improving communication skills—it’s about preventing errors, increasing efficiency, and building trust in professional settings. Whether you’re debugging code, leading a team, or designing software, the ability to understand before responding sets the best apart from the rest.

Ready to Strengthen Your Communication Skills?

Implementing Habit 5: Seek First to Understand, Then to Be Understood is more than just a mindset shift—it’s a career game-changer. Whether you’re working in IT leadership, cybersecurity, UX design, or software development, mastering empathic listening will help you:

  • Improve team collaboration by fostering trust and clarity in communication.
  • Reduce errors and inefficiencies in software development and problem-solving.
  • Enhance customer support by truly understanding user pain points before responding.
  • Become a stronger leader by listening to your team’s needs and guiding them effectively.

Start practicing today! The next time you’re in a conversation—whether with a client, a colleague, or a stakeholder—pause before responding. Listen first. Ask thoughtful questions. Reflect on what was said. Then, and only then, craft your response.
